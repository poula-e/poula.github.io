Ancient Egyptian Clothing

The clothing used in the ancient world reflects the techniques that these peoples mastered, and antiquities play a major role in documenting this aspect of ancient life. Silk, fabrics, and leather are sometimes well preserved over time. The clothes worn in many cultures indicate social level. Fashion in clothing is one of human characteristics and is a feature of most human societies. In very ancient days, humans began to wear clothes to protect their bodies from heat, sun, rain, cold, and others. The skins of animals and plants were mainly used as clothing to cover their bodies.

1. Royal Class

The royal costume in ancient Egypt was the most expensive and precious of costumes, and it was decorated with decorations woven with colored or embroidered threads, or by adding pieces of fabric to the ends of the garment, rimmed with gold or with single-colored or multi-colored ribbons. As for the traditional costume of kings, the painting of King Narmer is the oldest historical document of the king wearing a kilt. It was a long or short skirt with pleats, and it was called the “shandet.” Its ends intersected and folded under a belt tied in the front. The head covering was a piece of linen cloth gathered behind the head. The ancient Egyptian wore a false beard to strengthen the connection between terrorism, as they believed that gods had a straight beard, and kings wore Knitted abaya and wig for official exams.

2. Upper class

The ministers wore a tight, woven dress that descended from the chest to the feet, carried by a ribbon fastened at the back at the neck. As for the kilt, as in some statesmen, they wore a short, transparent kilt and a shirt folded over the shoulders. Some clothes in the form of nets were also discovered, made according to the macramé style. They were also distinguished by the wearing of jewelry. And jewelry: they would put bracelets on the wrist, rings on the fingers, necklaces, and necklaces consisting of five or six rows of colored beads, and as evidence of the importance of the position, they would place a shawl on the shoulders.

3. Middle Class

They wore simple, pleated clothes with a wide opening at the neck that fit the upper part of the body and widened at the end of the dress. Above the dress was a wide belt made of a pleated shawl of the same type of fabric as the dress, and its end ended in the form of a towel. Some wanted unadorned clothes with straps that extended the length of the body.

4. Lower Class

It was completely different from the dress of the upper classes, as their fashion was usually late in its development. In the Middle Kingdom era: Junior officials wore the short kilt of the old state. In the era of the New Kingdom: They wore the longest kilt of the Central State, while the people, which consisted of peasants, workers, shepherds, and craftsmen, were always content with simple and practical clothing, which consisted of a loincloth or trousers (mes) with a belt the size of a hand without any ornaments or embellishments, and a kilt (rojo), and he remained bareheaded. Barefoot. Workers in the era of the New Kingdom wore a leather net over their linen kilt, and the places above the legs were reinforced with a large patch.

5. Army and Police

The police uniform differed depending on the ranks and jobs. The shooters in the army wore a short red kilt with green decorations, and they wore necklaces or white hair ribbons wrapped around the head, expressing power and government.

6. Servants

They differed depending on the ranks of servants, but most of the time they wore a long kilt sometimes, sometimes a short kilt, some poorly made necklaces and bracelets, a bare upper part and chest area, and a hair wig wrapped with a tie on the back so as not to hinder the servant while he was working.

Class Containing
Royal class Pharaoh and his family
Upper class Ministers, high priests, scribes, and regional governors
Middle class Young employees, merchants, craftsmen and distinguished people
Lower Class Agricultural workers, artisans and shepherds
